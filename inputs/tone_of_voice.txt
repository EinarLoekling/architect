## Aristotle's pillars of persuasion

Around 350 BCE, Aristotle was hanging out in ancient Greece, watching orators do their thing in the 

agora, and he noticed something: the most persuasive speakers weren't just the smartest ones. 

They were the ones who nailed three things at once. 

He called them ethos, pathos, and logos - and they remain the blueprint for credibility today.

![Aristotle walking us through the credibility framework, 350 BCE (colorized, dramatized, probably historically inaccurate).](attachment:4ab45abc-f36f-4e3a-bd9e-e6cce028f42b:Gemini_Generated_Image_jwlfg2jwlfg2jwlf.png)

Let's break them down in language Aristotle definitely didn't use:

### 1. Logos (competence): Do you actually know what you're talking about?

Logos is the logic pillar. 

It's your perceived expertise - the "this person seems like they know their shit" factor.

This is the one most people obsess over, and for good reason. 

If you're a surgeon who's shaky on which side the heart is on, or a financial advisor who thinks a 401(k) is a type of sports car, you're toast. 

Competence is table stakes.

But here's the catch: you don't need to know everything. 

You just need to know enough, communicate it clearly, and demonstrate that you understand the 

boundaries of your knowledge. 

Saying "I don't know, but here's how I'd find out" is often more credible than bullshitting your way 

through an answer.

Logos is built through:

- Demonstrated expertise: Showing your work, not just your conclusions
- Pattern recognition: Connecting dots in ways others can't
- Clarity: Explaining complex things simply (without being condescending)
- Intellectual humility: Admitting what you don't know

In the old information economy, logos was enough. 

You knew things, so people paid you. 

Done.

In the credibility economy? 

Logos is just one leg of a three-legged stool. 

And a one-legged stool is just a fancy way to fall on your ass.

### 2. Ethos (character): Are you a shady bastard, or nah?

Ethos is the moral pillar. 

It's about your character - whether people believe your intentions are good, or whether they think 

you're just trying to get something out of them.

You can be the smartest person alive, but if people think you're manipulative, self-serving, or 

ethically squishy, your credibility evaporates. 

This is why so many slick salespeople lose trust.

They have the brains and the charm, but everyone can feel when the goal isn‚Äôt to help - it‚Äôs to close.

![One sells a product. The other sells trust.](attachment:52fa5425-433a-4e8b-885f-b08c1288382f:Gemini_Generated_Image_pakespakespakesp.png)

One sells a product. The other sells trust.

Ethos asks:

- Are you honest? Do you tell the truth even when it's inconvenient?
- Are you consistent? Do your actions match your words?
- Are you transparent about your incentives? If you're being paid to say something, do you admit it?
- Do you care about outcomes beyond your own benefit? Or are you just here for the bag?

Building ethos is slow and fragile. 

It takes years to build and seconds to destroy. 

One lie, one shady move, one "well actually I'm sponsored by..." reveal at the wrong time, and poof - 

credibility gone.

But when you have it? 

People will follow you into the uncertainty, trust your judgment when they don't have all the 

information, and give you the benefit of the doubt when things go sideways.

---

### 3. Pathos (connection): Do you get me?

Pathos is the emotional pillar. 

It's about connection - whether people feel like you understand them, see them, and actually give a 

damn about their perspective.

![When your ICP just wants connection, but you give them calculus.](attachment:1e432a06-8e1d-4c2c-b68d-dff45e7950f1:Gemini_Generated_Image_8j4sly8j4sly8j4s_(2).png)

When your ICP just wants connection, but you give them calculus.

This is the one most "smart" people ignore, and it's why so many brilliant experts have zero 

influence. 

They‚Äôre so focused on being right that they forget to make people feel understood - and to frame  things in terms of what actually matters to them.

Pathos answers:

- Do you understand my perspective? Not just intellectually, but emotionally?
- Do you speak my language? Or are you talking in jargon I need a decoder ring for?
- Do I feel seen by you? Or am I just another case study in your deck?
- Do you care about my experience? Or are you just here to flex your knowledge?

Pathos is why your therapist can say something you've heard a thousand times and it finally lands. 

It's why a tweet from a stranger sometimes hits harder than advice from your parents. 

It's why people trust their hairdresser more than their financial advisor.

Because connection creates permission. 

When people feel understood, they let you in. 

They drop their guard. 

They're willing to hear hard truths, change their minds, and take action on your advice.

Without pathos, you're just shouting information into the void - no matter how right you are.

# How to build credibility at scale

Here‚Äôs something important to remember: credibility is 100% perception. 

You can be the most trustworthy human alive - your wife can think you‚Äôre a genius; your kids can swear you‚Äôre a hero - but in business, credibility only exists where it‚Äôs seen.

So to build credibility at scale, you need consistent public signals of logos, pathos, and ethos.

That's it. That's the whole thing.

You pick a channel (LinkedIn, Twitter, newsletter, podcast, wherever your audience is). 

You show up consistently. 

And every time you show up, you're emitting signals across one or more pillars.

![Gemini_Generated_Image_xv0p7kxv0p7kxv0p.png](attachment:6ee76ebe-cb59-43c2-ad80-5775c3e21d91:Gemini_Generated_Image_xv0p7kxv0p7kxv0p.png)

Any piece of content can emit multiple signals:

A framework post can show:

- Logos (you understand the system)
- Ethos (you're giving it away for free)
- Pathos (you acknowledge where people actually struggle with it)

A personal story can show:

- Pathos (vulnerability, relatability)
- Logos (pattern recognition from experience)
- Ethos (honesty about what you fucked up)

A strong opinion can show:

- Logos (independent thinking, clear reasoning)
- Ethos (courage to say something unpopular)
- Pathos (naming what others are afraid to say)

The question isn't "what should I post?"

It's "what signals am I sending?"

Most people optimize for one pillar at a time and wonder why nothing sticks:

- The expert who shares knowledge but has zero personality (logos, no pathos)
- The relatable storyteller who can't back up their claims (pathos, no logos)
- The earnest do-gooder who's boring as hell (ethos, no pathos or logos)

You need all three, firing simultaneously, repeatedly, until the pattern becomes undeniable.
